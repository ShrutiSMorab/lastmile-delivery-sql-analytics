# Last-Mile Delivery Network Analytics

A SQLite case study on a six-station delivery network: 287,635 packages, 308,361 delivery attempts, 339 drivers, over roughly twelve weeks. I built it to answer the operational questions I deal with day to day in Amazon Logistics, using SQL rather than spreadsheets.

## What the analysis showed

**The network is running out of room.** Five of six stations sit above 90% utilisation, and three are already over capacity on average daily volume. At current growth rates, only one station has more than a week of headroom left.

| Station | Daily capacity | Current daily average | Utilisation | Weeks until capacity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| BER-4 | 700 | 1,007 | 143.8% | 0 |
| BER-1 | 900 | 919 | 102.1% | 0 |
| MUC-1 | 850 | 843 | 99.2% | 0 |
| HAM-2 | 800 | 777 | 97.1% | 1 |
| CGN-3 | 650 | 603 | 92.7% | 1 |
| LEJ-1 | 500 | 451 | 90.3% | 6 |

**BER-4 is the problem, and it is the problem on every measure.** It carries the most volume against the smallest capacity, and it is also the fastest growing station at 4.94% week on week. It has the worst first-attempt success rate at 90.0%, the highest route overrun at 14%, and the highest reattempt cost at roughly €15,080 across the period.

**Overloaded stations deliver worse.** Ranking stations by utilisation produces almost the same order as ranking them by first-attempt failure. BER-4 at 143.8% utilisation delivers 90.0% first time. LEJ-1 at 90.3% delivers 94.1%. The gap is small in percentage terms but it runs across the whole network in the same direction, which suggests the performance problem is a capacity problem wearing a different hat.

**Most failures are not the driver's fault.** Customer not home accounts for 46.9% of failed attempts and access problems for another 23.4%. Together that is 70% of all failures, and neither is fixed by driver training. Access problems are concentrated in urban zones, which take 84% of them despite carrying well under half the network's stations.

**Driver tenure barely moves the number.** Rookies under 90 days deliver 90.7% first time. Drivers with over a year deliver 92.99%. A 2.3 point gap across the whole tenure curve is real but small, and it reinforces the point above: this network's constraint is structural, not individual.

**Saturday is the best delivery day.** Failure rate drops to 6.25% against 7.2% to 7.5% on weekdays, which is consistent with the not-home finding since more people are in.

## What I would do with this

If I were presenting this to an operations lead, the recommendation would not be a driver performance programme. It would be capacity at BER-4 first, since it is simultaneously the most overloaded, the worst performing and the fastest growing, and then delivery window or access interventions aimed at the 70% of failures that are customer-side rather than driver-side.

Reattempts across the network cost roughly €51,800 over the period at an assumed €2.50 per attempt. BER-4 alone is 29% of that.

## What is in the repo

| File | Contents |
|---|---|
| `00_schema.sql` | Five table schema: stations, drivers, routes, packages, delivery attempts |
| `01_data_quality_checks.sql` | Orphan records, referential integrity, contradictory status flags |
| `02_network_kpis.sql` | First-attempt success, on-time rate, weekly volume, undelivered share |
| `03_station_performance.sql` | Station comparison, stops per route, route overrun, utilisation |
| `04_failure_analysis.sql` | Failure reasons, zone breakdown, weekday pattern, reattempt cost |
| `05_driver_tenure_analysis.sql` | Success by tenure bucket, top and bottom performers per station |
| `06_capacity_planning.sql` | Week on week growth and weeks until each station hits capacity |
| `query_outputs.txt` | Actual output from every query, so results can be checked against the SQL |
| `STUDY_WALKTHROUGH.md` | The structured plan I followed working through the queries |

The queries progress from basic aggregation to joins, CTEs and window functions.

## How it was built

The dataset is synthetic. I generated it with AI assistance so I would have a realistic sandbox to work in, and I designed the schema and the business questions around the operational world I already know: stations, delivery windows, route capacity, package flow. I write and run the queries myself.

Tools: SQLite via `sqlite3` on macOS Terminal, and DB Browser for SQLite.

## Limitations

- The data is generated, not real. The patterns in it are ones I built in, so the findings demonstrate analytical method rather than discovery.
- The €2.50 reattempt cost is an assumption I applied, not a figure from the data.
- Twelve weeks is a short window for the growth projections in `06`. I would want a full year before trusting weeks-until-capacity as a planning number.
- No cost or revenue data, so none of this speaks to profitability per route.

## Run it yourself

```bash
sqlite3 lastmile.db < 00_schema.sql
sqlite3 lastmile.db < 02_network_kpis.sql
```
